reform_2019:
  heading: The 2019 reform fell short.
  body: >
    The 2019 no-fault overhaul banned a list of non-driving rating
    factors — credit, education, occupation, ZIP code as a *sole*
    basis — and promised lower premiums in Detroit.


    Statewide premiums fell about 18%. In Detroit, they fell less
    than half that. Insurers shifted from ZIP-based rating to
    census-tract and "garaging address" rating that tracks the same
    racial geography. The 2019 reform's language was real, but the
    enforcement was thin and the workaround was easy.


    The next bill needs to close the loopholes the last bill left.

pillars:
  heading: The four-part program.
  lede: >
    Ordered by political feasibility and evidence base. The strongest
    plank is first.
  items:
    - number: "01"
      heading: Disparate-impact testing for every rating factor.
      body: >
        Modeled on Colorado SB21-169. Insurers must test their rating
        factors for disparate impact on protected classes and justify
        any disparity to the regulator. **Already working in Colorado.**
        Doesn't ban anything outright. Survives constitutional
        scrutiny. This is the strongest, most defensible plank.
    - number: "02"
      heading: Ban the proxies the 2019 reform left intact.
      body: >
        Credit-based insurance scores. Education. Occupation. Prior
        insurance gaps. Homeownership. The 2019 reform banned several
        of these as the *sole* basis for rating, but they remain
        permitted as one factor among many — which means they remain
        in use. Close the loophole.
    - number: "03"
      heading: Cap territorial granularity.
      body: >
        Today insurers can carve "territories" as fine as a census
        tract. That's how the 2019 ZIP ban was neutralized — the
        rating just moved one level finer. A statutory floor on
        territory size, plus commissioner approval of territory
        definitions, restores the integrity of the 2019 framework.
    - number: "04"
      heading: Mandatory rate-factor disclosure.
      body: >
        Consumers should be able to see what rating factors were
        applied to their quote. The regulator should publish the
        factor distributions across territories. Cheap, popular,
        doesn't disrupt the market — and makes future advocacy
        possible. Birny Birnbaum at the Center for Economic Justice
        has been arguing for this for a decade.
